Term Sheet Explainer

A plain-English guide for Indian startup founders

What is a Term Sheet?

A term sheet is a 1-5 page document that outlines the key terms of a proposed investment. Think of it as a letter of intent — it captures what the investor wants before lawyers draft the detailed agreements (SHA and SSA).

Key thing to know: Most clauses in a term sheet are non-binding. The investor is saying "here's what we'd like" — you can negotiate. However, two clauses are almost always binding: exclusivity (no-shop) and confidentiality. Once you sign, you can't shop the deal to other investors during the exclusivity period.

In India, term sheets follow broadly similar patterns whether the investor is an Indian VC (Sequoia India / Peak XV, Accel, Matrix / Z47) or a foreign fund investing via an offshore structure. The legal agreements that follow will reference the Companies Act 2013, FEMA regulations (for foreign investors), and SEBI rules.

Valuation Basics

Pre-money, post-money, price per share, and how ESOP dilution works.

Valuation determines how much of your company you're giving away. The two numbers that matter are pre-money valuation (what your company is worth before the investment) and post-money valuation (pre-money + investment amount).

Post-money = Pre-money + Investment
Investor ownership = Investment / Post-money

Example:
Pre-money: ₹40 Cr  |  Investment: ₹10 Cr
Post-money: ₹40 Cr + ₹10 Cr = ₹50 Cr
Investor gets: ₹10 Cr / ₹50 Cr = 20%
Standard valuation terms Standard

Pre-money valuation is stated clearly. Price per share is calculated by dividing pre-money by the fully-diluted share count (including ESOP). Both parties agree on the cap table before and after the round.

Example: "Pre-money valuation of ₹40 Cr on a fully-diluted basis, including an ESOP pool of 10%. Investment amount of ₹10 Cr for 20% of the post-money equity."
ESOP pool inflated pre-money Aggressive

Investor asks to expand ESOP from 5% to 15% before the round — this dilutes only founders, not the investor. The "real" pre-money is lower than it looks.

Example: "Pre-money valuation of ₹40 Cr including a 15% ESOP pool (expanded from current 5%)." The 10% expansion comes entirely out of founder equity. Effective pre-money for founders is closer to ₹34 Cr.
Valuation subject to diligence adjustment Unusual

A clause that allows the investor to reduce the valuation after signing the term sheet based on diligence findings. This effectively makes the valuation meaningless — you've given exclusivity for a number that can change.

Example: "Pre-money valuation of ₹40 Cr, subject to downward adjustment based on findings during due diligence." This is a red flag — the term sheet locks you in, but the investor's commitment is fluid.
Why it matters: Valuation directly determines how much of your company you own after the round. A seemingly high valuation with a bloated ESOP pool or diligence-adjustment clause can mean you're giving away more than you think. Always calculate your actual ownership percentage post-round, not just the headline number.

Liquidation Preference

What investors get paid first when the company is sold or shut down.

Liquidation preference determines the order in which money is distributed when there's an "exit" — a sale, merger, or winding up of the company. Investors with a preference get their money back before founders and employees see a rupee.

1x non-participating preference Standard

Investor gets back 1x their investment or their pro-rata share of the exit proceeds — whichever is higher. They choose one, not both. This is the founder-friendly standard.

Example: Investor put in ₹10 Cr for 20%. Company sells for ₹100 Cr.
Option A: 1x preference = ₹10 Cr
Option B: 20% of ₹100 Cr = ₹20 Cr
Investor picks Option B (₹20 Cr). Founders get ₹80 Cr. Fair outcome.
1x participating preference Aggressive

Investor gets 1x their money back and then also participates pro-rata in the remaining proceeds. This is "double-dipping" — they get downside protection plus upside participation.

Example: Investor put in ₹10 Cr for 20%. Company sells for ₹100 Cr.
Step 1: Investor gets ₹10 Cr off the top.
Step 2: Remaining ₹90 Cr split pro-rata. Investor gets 20% = ₹18 Cr.
Investor total: ₹28 Cr (not ₹20 Cr). Founders get ₹72 Cr instead of ₹80 Cr.
2x+ participating preference Unusual

Investor gets 2x (or more) their money back before anyone else sees anything, and participates in the remainder. This crushes founder returns in anything other than a massive exit.

Example: Investor put in ₹10 Cr for 20%. Company sells for ₹100 Cr.
Step 1: Investor gets 2x = ₹20 Cr off the top.
Step 2: Remaining ₹80 Cr split pro-rata. Investor gets 20% = ₹16 Cr.
Investor total: ₹36 Cr. Founders get ₹64 Cr. At a smaller ₹30 Cr exit, founders might get almost nothing.
Why it matters: Liquidation preference is the single most important economic term after valuation. In a modest exit (say 2-3x the post-money), a participating preference can take a huge chunk of founder returns. Always model your exit scenarios with actual numbers — don't just negotiate the headline valuation.

Anti-Dilution Protection

What happens to the investor's price if you raise a future round at a lower valuation (a "down round").

Anti-dilution clauses protect investors if the company raises money in the future at a lower price per share than they paid. The investor's conversion price is adjusted downward, effectively giving them more shares for the same money — at the expense of founders.

Broad-based weighted average Standard

The investor's conversion price is adjusted using a weighted average formula that considers the size of the down round relative to the total shares outstanding. A small down round causes only a small adjustment. This is the market standard and is fair to both sides.

Example: Investor bought at ₹100/share. Down round at ₹60/share, but only 10% of total shares are issued in the down round. The weighted average formula adjusts the investor's price to roughly ₹95/share — a modest correction, not a windfall.
Narrow-based weighted average Aggressive

Same formula as broad-based, but uses a smaller denominator (only preferred shares, not all shares). This produces a larger price adjustment, giving the investor more additional shares in a down round.

Example: Same scenario as above, but the narrow base means the investor's price adjusts to roughly ₹85/share instead of ₹95 — significantly more dilution for founders.
Full ratchet Unusual

The investor's conversion price drops to the exact price of the down round, regardless of how small the down round is. Even a tiny bridge round at a lower price reprices the entire previous investment.

Example: Investor bought ₹10 Cr at ₹100/share (1 lakh shares). A small bridge round prices at ₹60/share. Full ratchet adjusts the investor to ₹60/share — they now effectively hold 1.67 lakh shares. Founders are massively diluted even though the down round was small.
Why it matters: Anti-dilution determines how much pain founders bear in a down round. Broad-based weighted average is fair and market-standard. Full ratchet can be devastating — it turns every small stumble into a major founder dilution event. If an investor insists on full ratchet, treat it as a serious red flag about their intentions.

Board Composition & Protective Provisions

Who controls the board, and what decisions require investor approval.

Board composition determines who has a seat at the table for major decisions. Protective provisions (also called "reserved matters" or "affirmative vote rights") list specific actions that require investor consent — even if the board votes in favor.

Founder-majority board with reasonable reserved matters Standard

Board has 3-5 seats, founders hold the majority. Investor gets 1 board seat (or 1 per lead investor). Reserved matters are limited to capital-protection issues: new share issuance, changes to share rights, related-party transactions above a threshold, sale of the company.

Example: 3-member board: 2 founder-nominees, 1 investor-nominee. Reserved matters require investor consent for: issuing new shares, changing the Articles, any M&A transaction, related-party transactions above ₹25 lakhs, taking debt above ₹1 Cr.
Investor-heavy board or broad reserved matters Aggressive

Investor gets 2 seats on a 5-member board (with 1 "independent" director they influence), or reserved matters extend to operational decisions like hiring key employees, changing business plans, or entering new markets.

Example: 5-member board: 2 founders, 2 investors, 1 "mutually agreed independent." Reserved matters include: hiring/firing any employee above ₹30 LPA, annual budgets, any pivot in business model, any contract above ₹50 lakhs. This gives the investor a veto on day-to-day operations.
Quorum trap or investor-majority board Unusual

Board quorum requires the investor nominee to be present — meaning no board meeting can happen without the investor. Or the investor gets outright majority on the board. Either way, you've lost control of your own company.

Example: "Board quorum shall require the presence of at least one Investor Director." If the investor stops showing up, your board is paralyzed — no resolutions, no bank signatories, no statutory filings. This is a governance trap.
Why it matters: Losing board control at a seed or Series A stage is premature. Broad reserved matters turn every operational decision into a negotiation. And quorum traps can paralyze your company if the relationship sours. Keep reserved matters to genuine capital-protection issues, and ensure you can hold valid board meetings independently.

ESOP Pool

How the employee stock option pool affects founder dilution.

Almost every term sheet will require an ESOP (Employee Stock Option Pool) to attract and retain talent. The key question is: who bears the dilution — everyone proportionally, or only the founders?

Pre-money ESOP carve-out (dilutes only founders):
Pre-money ₹40 Cr, ESOP 10%, Investment ₹10 Cr
Founders effectively own: 90% of pre-money = ₹36 Cr / ₹50 Cr post = 72%
Investor owns: ₹10 Cr / ₹50 Cr = 20%
ESOP: 10% × (₹40 Cr / ₹50 Cr) = 8%

Post-money ESOP (dilutes everyone equally):
Pre-money ₹40 Cr, Investment ₹10 Cr, then 10% ESOP carved out
Founders own: 80% × 90% = 72% — same here, but investor also dilutes to 18%
Reasonable ESOP pool (10-15%) already included Standard

ESOP pool of 10-15% is included in the pre-money valuation, and the current pool already covers most of it. Only a small top-up is needed. The cap table is clear about how this affects everyone.

Example: "Pre-money of ₹40 Cr on a fully-diluted basis, including the existing ESOP pool of 8% to be topped up to 12%." Only 4% additional dilution from the existing cap table.
Large ESOP expansion pre-money Aggressive

Investor asks to expand ESOP from 5% to 20% — all pre-money. The 15% expansion comes entirely out of founder equity. The investor's percentage is unaffected.

Example: "ESOP pool to be expanded to 20% pre-money (currently 5%)." This 15% expansion at a ₹40 Cr pre-money is equivalent to a ₹6 Cr valuation cut for founders — a hidden discount the investor gets for free.
Unallocated ESOP with investor veto on grants Unusual

A large ESOP pool is created but requires investor approval for every grant. The unallocated shares sit in the pool diluting founders, while the investor controls who gets options and when.

Example: "20% ESOP pool, all grants require prior written consent of Investor." Founders bear the dilution but don't control the allocation. If you and the investor disagree about a key hire's package, you're stuck.
Why it matters: ESOP is essential to hire great people, but an oversized pre-money pool is a hidden valuation discount for the investor. Negotiate the pool size based on your actual hiring plan for the next 18-24 months, not the investor's wish list. And always retain control over individual grant decisions.

Founder Vesting & Lock-in

How your own shares can be at risk if you leave the company.

Investor-imposed "reverse vesting" means that even though you already own your shares, you agree to give some back if you leave before a certain period. This protects the investor from a founder walking away with a large stake. It's reasonable in principle but the details matter enormously.

3-4 year vesting, single-trigger acceleration Standard

Founder shares vest over 3-4 years with a 1-year cliff. Credit given for time already spent building the company. Acceleration on change-of-control (if the company is acquired, your shares fully vest). Good leaver/bad leaver distinction is clear.

Example: "Founder shares vest over 4 years from the date of incorporation (giving credit for 18 months already served). 1-year cliff, monthly vesting thereafter. 100% acceleration on change of control. Good leaver: retains all vested shares."
Vesting restarts, narrow good-leaver definition Aggressive

Vesting clock starts from the date of investment (ignoring prior years of work). "Good leaver" is defined very narrowly — even being fired without cause might trigger "bad leaver" provisions with a forced buyback at nominal value.

Example: "Founder shares vest over 4 years from the date of this Agreement. Bad leaver: Company has the right to buyback all unvested shares at face value (₹10/share)." If you've been building for 2 years, the clock resets and you're treated as if you just started.
Vesting on vested shares, personal penalty clauses Unusual

Even shares you've already "vested" can be clawed back. Or the bad leaver clause requires you to sell shares at face value (₹10/share) instead of fair market value. Personal financial penalties beyond share forfeiture.

Example: "Bad leaver (including voluntary resignation for any reason): all shares (vested and unvested) to be transferred to the Company at par value." This means if you resign after 3 years to start something new, you lose everything — even shares you rightfully earned.
Why it matters: Vesting terms determine what happens to your single largest asset if your relationship with the investor sours, or if you're asked to leave. Insist on credit for time served, a clear good-leaver definition (at minimum: termination without cause, disability, death), and buyback at fair market value — never face value.

Non-Compete & Non-Solicitation

Restrictions on what you can do during and after your time at the company.

Indian law context: Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 makes agreements "in restraint of trade" void. This means post-termination non-competes are generally unenforceable in India (unlike the US). However, during-employment non-competes and non-solicitation clauses are valid, and investors still include post-termination restrictions as a deterrent.

During-term non-compete, reasonable non-solicit Standard

Non-compete applies during your employment only. Non-solicitation of employees/customers for 12 months post-departure. Both limited to the company's specific business domain.

Example: "During the Founder's engagement with the Company: shall not engage in any Competing Business. For 12 months post-departure: shall not solicit any employee or customer of the Company."
Broad post-termination non-compete Aggressive

Non-compete extends 2+ years post-departure and covers a broad definition of "competing business." While likely unenforceable under Section 27, litigation risk and the cost of defending yourself can be a deterrent.

Example: "For 24 months after departure, Founder shall not directly or indirectly engage in, advise, or invest in any business in the technology sector in India." The definition of "technology sector" is so broad it could cover almost anything.
Non-compete tied to share forfeiture or penalty Unusual

Violating the non-compete triggers a financial penalty or forces you to sell your vested shares at nominal value. This makes the otherwise-unenforceable non-compete de facto enforceable — the penalty is in the shareholder agreement, not the employment contract.

Example: "Breach of non-compete by Founder shall be deemed a Bad Leaver event, requiring transfer of all shares at par value, plus liquidated damages of ₹1 Cr." Even though Section 27 says the non-compete is void, the linked financial penalty creates real enforcement power.
Why it matters: While Indian courts generally won't enforce post-employment non-competes, investors use indirect enforcement mechanisms (share forfeiture, liquidated damages) to achieve the same effect. Pay close attention to the linkage between non-compete breach and your shares. Non-solicitation is generally enforceable and reasonable — focus your negotiation energy on the non-compete terms.

Conditions Precedent

What must happen before the investment actually closes.

Conditions Precedent (CPs) are a list of things that must be done before the investor is obligated to wire the money. Some are standard (board approvals, regulatory filings). Others can be open-ended escape hatches that let the investor walk away even after signing the term sheet.

Specific, achievable CPs Standard

CPs are concrete, within the company's control, and have a clear timeline. Examples: board approval, shareholder approval, conversion of company to private limited (if needed), opening of a designated bank account.

Example: "CPs: (1) Board resolution approving the transaction, (2) Shareholders' resolution for allotment of CCPS, (3) Filing of MGT-14 with ROC, (4) KYC documents of the Company. Long-stop date: 45 days from execution."
Vague or subjective CPs Aggressive

CPs include "satisfactory completion of due diligence" or "no material adverse change" — subjective conditions that the investor can use to delay or exit the deal.

Example: "CPs: (1) Completion of legal, financial, and tax due diligence to the satisfaction of the Investor, (2) No Material Adverse Effect on the Company." "Satisfaction of the Investor" is entirely subjective — they can always find something unsatisfactory.
CPs that require third-party action Unusual

CPs include things outside your control — regulatory approvals that may take months, consent from parties who have no obligation to give it, or resolution of pending litigation.

Example: "CPs: (1) RBI approval for foreign investment (if applicable), (2) Resolution of all pending litigation, (3) Consent of all existing shareholders including angel investors." Any of these can stall the deal indefinitely, and the investor bears no risk during the wait.
Why it matters: Vague CPs give the investor an option, not an obligation. You've given them exclusivity, you've told the market you're raising, your other investors know — and the investor can still walk away citing an unsatisfied CP. Push for specific, time-bound CPs and a short long-stop date (30-45 days).

Exclusivity / No-Shop

The binding clause that prevents you from talking to other investors.

The exclusivity (or "no-shop") clause is typically binding even though the rest of the term sheet is non-binding. During the exclusivity period, you cannot solicit, negotiate with, or provide information to other potential investors. This is reasonable — the investor needs time to do diligence without worrying you'll take a better offer. But the duration matters.

30-45 days exclusivity Standard

A 30-45 day exclusivity window gives the investor enough time to complete diligence and draft definitive agreements. Automatically expires — no extension without mutual consent.

Example: "For a period of 30 days from the date of this Term Sheet, the Company and the Founders shall not solicit, encourage, or enter into discussions with any third party regarding any financing transaction."
60-90 days or auto-extending exclusivity Aggressive

Longer exclusivity means more time where you can't pursue alternatives. Auto-extension clauses (e.g., "extends by 30 days if diligence is ongoing") can lock you in for months.

Example: "Exclusivity of 60 days, automatically extended by 30 days if due diligence has not been completed." This can easily become 90+ days — nearly a quarter of the year where your fundraise is on hold.
Exclusivity with penalties or break fees Unusual

A financial penalty or "break fee" if you terminate exclusivity or take another offer. This is extremely unusual for venture deals in India and creates an asymmetric burden — the investor can walk away from the non-binding term sheet, but you can't.

Example: "If the Company breaches exclusivity, the Company shall reimburse the Investor for all diligence costs (up to ₹25 lakhs) and pay a break fee of ₹50 lakhs." This turns a non-binding term sheet into a quasi-binding commitment for the founder.
Why it matters: Time is your scarcest resource during a fundraise. A long exclusivity window means your other conversations go cold, your team is distracted, and the investor has all the leverage. Keep it to 30-45 days, resist auto-extensions, and never accept a break fee — the term sheet is supposed to be non-binding for both sides.
