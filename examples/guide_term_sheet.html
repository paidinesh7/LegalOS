Term Sheet Explainer

A plain-English guide for Indian startup founders

What is a Term Sheet?

A 1-5 page non-binding document outlining the key terms of a proposed investment — think of it as a letter of intent before lawyers draft the SHA and SSA. Most clauses are negotiable, but exclusivity and confidentiality are binding from day one.

Fundraise Timeline

Where the term sheet fits in a typical Indian startup fundraise.

1

Pitch & Diligence

You pitch investors, they do preliminary diligence on your business, market, and team.

2

Term Sheet ← you are here

Investor issues a term sheet. You negotiate terms. Both sides sign the non-binding term sheet.

3

Due Diligence

Investor's lawyers conduct legal, financial, and tax diligence on your company.

4

Definitive Agreements

Lawyers draft the SHA (Shareholders' Agreement) and SSA (Share Subscription Agreement). These are binding.

5

Closing

CPs are satisfied, documents are signed, money hits the bank account, shares are allotted.

Valuation Basics

Valuation determines how much of your company you're giving away — pre-money + investment = post-money.

Post-money = Pre-money + Investment
Investor ownership = Investment / Post-money

Example:
Pre-money: ₹40 Cr  |  Investment: ₹10 Cr
Post-money: ₹50 Cr  |  Investor gets: 20%

Always calculate your actual post-round ownership including ESOP dilution. A high headline valuation with a bloated ESOP pool or diligence-adjustment clause hides the real price.

Liquidation Preference

Determines payout order when the company is sold — investors with preference get paid before founders see a rupee.

What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
Standard1x non-participating — higher of 1x return or pro-rata share₹10 Cr for 20%. Exit ₹100 Cr → investor takes ₹20 Cr (pro-rata wins)Fair — investor picks the better of two options, not both
Aggressive1x participating — gets 1x back plus pro-rata of remainderSame deal. Exit ₹100 Cr → ₹10 Cr + 20% of ₹90 Cr = ₹28 CrDouble-dipping. Founders get ₹72 Cr instead of ₹80 Cr
Unusual2x+ participating — gets 2x back plus pro-rata of remainderSame deal. Exit ₹100 Cr → ₹20 Cr + 20% of ₹80 Cr = ₹36 CrFounders get ₹64 Cr. At ₹30 Cr exit, founders get almost nothing

Model your exit at 2x, 5x, and 10x post-money. Participating preference crushes founder returns in modest exits.

Anti-Dilution Protection

Protects investors if a future round prices lower (a "down round") by adjusting their conversion price — at founders' expense.

What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
StandardBroad-based weighted average — adjustment proportional to down-round sizeBought at ₹100/share, down round at ₹60 (10% of shares). Adjusts to ~₹95Small, fair correction
AggressiveNarrow-based weighted average — smaller denominator, bigger adjustmentSame scenario. Adjusts to ~₹85 instead of ₹95Significantly more dilution for founders
UnusualFull ratchet — conversion price drops to the new round's price entirelySame scenario. Adjusts to ₹60. 1L shares become 1.67L sharesEven a tiny bridge round causes massive founder dilution

Broad-based weighted average is market standard. Full ratchet turns every small stumble into a major dilution event — treat it as a red flag.

ESOP Pool

The employee stock option pool dilutes someone — the key question is whether it dilutes everyone equally or only founders.

Pre-money ESOP carve-out (dilutes only founders):
Pre-money ₹40 Cr, ESOP 10%, Investment ₹10 Cr
Founders effectively own: 90% of ₹40 Cr / ₹50 Cr = 72%
Investor owns: 20%  |  ESOP: 8%

Post-money ESOP (dilutes everyone):
Same numbers but ESOP carved after investment
Founders: 72%  |  Investor: 18%  |  ESOP: 10%
What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
Standard10-15% pool, mostly existing, small top-up neededExisting 8% ESOP → top-up to 12%. Only 4% new dilutionRight-sized for 18-24 month hiring plan
AggressiveLarge expansion pre-money (5% → 20%) — investor's % unaffected15% expansion at ₹40 Cr pre-money = ~₹6 Cr hidden valuation cutAll dilution falls on founders only
UnusualLarge pool with investor veto on every individual grant20% pool created but all grants need investor written consentYou bear the dilution but don't control allocation

Size the ESOP pool to your actual hiring plan, not the investor's wish list. Always retain control over individual grant decisions.

Founder Vesting & Lock-in

"Reverse vesting" means you can lose your own shares if you leave before a set period — the details matter enormously.

What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
Standard3-4 yr vest with credit for time already served. Acceleration on acquisition4 yr from incorporation (credit 18 months). 100% accelerate on change of control. Good leaver retains vested sharesFair — recognizes prior work, clear good-leaver at FMV
AggressiveVesting restarts from investment date. Narrow good-leaver definition4 yr from signing — 2 years of prior work ignored. Bad leaver buyback at ₹10/share (par value)Clock reset erases your sweat-equity contribution
UnusualClawback of already-vested shares. All shares at par value on departureResign after 3 years → all shares (vested + unvested) transferred at ₹10/shareYou lose everything if you leave for any reason

Insist on credit for time served, a clear good-leaver definition (at minimum: termination without cause, disability, death), and buyback at FMV — never par value.

Non-Compete & Non-Solicitation

Restrictions on what you can do during and after your time at the company. Post-employment non-competes are generally void under Section 27, Indian Contract Act — but investors use indirect enforcement.

What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
StandardDuring-term non-compete only. 12-month non-solicit post-departureNo competing business while employed. 12-month employee/customer non-solicitReasonable and legally enforceable
Aggressive24-month post-departure non-compete, broad "technology sector" scopeCan't work in "technology" for 2 years. Likely unenforceable but litigation riskDeters you via legal costs even if you'd win
UnusualNon-compete breach triggers share forfeiture + liquidated damagesBreach = Bad Leaver (all shares at ₹10) + ₹1 Cr damagesMakes unenforceable clause de facto enforceable via financial penalty

During-term non-compete is fine. Resist any linkage between post-departure non-compete and share forfeiture — that's backdoor enforcement of an unenforceable clause.

Conditions Precedent

The to-do list before the investor must wire money — vague CPs are escape hatches that let the investor walk away.

What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
StandardSpecific CPs (board approval, filings) with 45-day long-stopBoard resolution, shareholder approval, MGT-14, KYC. Long-stop: 45 daysClear, achievable, time-bound
AggressiveSubjective CPs ("satisfactory diligence"), no long-stop date"Due diligence to investor's satisfaction" + "no material adverse change"Investor can delay or exit the deal at will
UnusualCPs requiring third-party action outside your control"Resolve all pending litigation" + "consent of all existing angels"Deal can stall indefinitely. Investor bears no risk while waiting

Push for specific, time-bound CPs and a 30-45 day long-stop date. Never accept "satisfactory diligence" as a CP — it's a free exit ramp for the investor.

Exclusivity / No-Shop

The binding clause that prevents you from talking to other investors — reasonable in principle, but duration is everything.

What It Looks Like₹ ExampleFounder Impact
Standard30-45 days, auto-expires, no extension30-day no-shop from term sheet date. Expires automaticallyEnough time for diligence, then you're free
Aggressive60-90 days with auto-extension clause60 days + 30-day auto-extension "if diligence ongoing"Locks you out of the market for a full quarter
UnusualExclusivity with break fee / diligence reimbursement90 days + ₹50 lakh break fee + ₹25 lakh diligence cost reimbursementNon-binding term sheet becomes quasi-binding for you only

30-45 days, no auto-extensions, no break fees. The term sheet is supposed to be non-binding for both sides — don't let exclusivity penalties change that.
